## Customer Slicing — narrow to a who-where pair

> Rule of thumb: *Good customer segments are a who-where pair. If you don't know where to find them, keep slicing until you do.*

A vague segment ("students", "advertisers", "sales orgs") is a trap: you end up having one conversation each with 20 different kinds of person and the feedback is contradictory. *If you aren't finding consistent problems and goals, your segment isn't specific enough yet.*

Take the broad segment and slice with these questions (repeat on any slice that's still generic):

1. Within this group, **who would want it most**?
2. Would **everyone** in the group buy/use it, or only some?
3. **Why** do they want it (problem or goal)?
4. Does everyone have that motivation, or only some?
5. What **other** motivations exist?
6. Which **other types of people** share those motivations?

Then attach behavior + location (this is what makes the segment *findable*):

7. What are these people **already doing** to cope with the problem (workaround behaviors)?
8. **Where** can we find the demographic groups?
9. **Where** can we find people doing those workaround behaviors?
10. Any group **un-findable**? Slice finer until you know where to find them.

Pick the starting slice that scores best on: **profitable · easy to reach · rewarding for you to serve**. (Illustrative slicing: "students" → "people scared of public speaking trying to get better" → found instantly at a Toastmasters meetup. The vague version had no door; the sliced version has many. The book's own worked case is powdered-superfood buyers sliced down to moms-with-young-kids who already shop at independent health-food stores.)

Also watch for **missed stakeholders**: marketplaces, kids+parents, B2B buying committees (user ≠ budget owner ≠ approver), and critical partners (manufacturing/distribution/promotion) are each their own segment with their own questions. Talk to people *representative of customers*, not the most senior people who look good on a status report.

*Saturation test (how to know a slice is tight enough):* interview until you stop hearing new things — as few as **3–5** conversations for a focused segment. If you're past **~10** and the answers are still all over the map, the persona is too fuzzy; go back and slice finer.

## Building Carol (the ICP / ideal customer persona)

> "Selling to everyone means selling to no one." Targeting one perfect customer paradoxically yields **10–100× more** total customers, because specific messaging is the only kind that resonates.

**Carol** is custom-built to be blown away by your product. Define her by:

- **Company type / industry** and **job title / role** (like your existing best customers).
- **Her problems in her own words** — "the way she'd describe them complaining to a friend over lunch." Not your framing.
- **Budget already allocated**, and she's **actively looking** to spend it *now*.
- **Which of your strengths she specifically needs**, and which of your weaknesses she actively doesn't care about.
- **What drives her retention** (why she'd stay a year later).

**Exclude** irrelevant backstory (hobbies, pets, taste in music). Only professional attributes, what she's interested in, *why* now, how she decides, and her budget.

**The bullseye (why specificity expands reach):**
- **Carol** (center) — values all your strengths, wants none of what you lack. Smallest ring.
- **Diana** (~10×) — generally values your positives, indifferent to your gaps.
- **Eddie** (~100× ring, ~10% adopt) — makes a normal pros/cons trade-off and still picks you *because* your positioning was clear.

How to find your real Carol from an existing business: ask Sales "when someone says ___, I know we'll close"; ask Support "the best customers are ___ because ___"; check retention data for who stays forever. **Cancellation rule:** a *Carol* who cancels is critical signal; an *Eddie* who cancels is noise — don't steer the product by it. Seasoned founders **turn down revenue** from non-ICP customers (the costly tech support, bespoke features, and churn aren't worth it) — "this separates first-time from seasoned founders."

## Is there really a problem? (Cohen's 6 conditions)

Solving a problem is not enough. A viable target must clear all six — each is an "and," so they multiply:

1. **Plausible** — market big enough (~10M+ consumers, or ~100k+ companies B2B).
2. **Self-aware** — they *know* they have the problem and *care* (vs. ignorance or "not top-3 priority").
3. **Lucrative** — they have **budget allocated** to this specific problem (consumers mostly won't pay; businesses spend to make/save money — self-funded founders should lean B2B).
4. **Liquid** — they can actually buy *now* (no multi-year contract lock-in, integration sunk cost, or compliance gauntlet blocking them).
5. **Eager** — they prefer *you* specifically (trust you'll survive + a real differentiator on something enough of them care about).
6. **Enduring** — they stay / renew / refer a year later (churn is exponential; it catches up to linear growth).

Use these to sanity-check the persona *before* writing questions — and note that Mom Test categories map directly onto them (implications→self-aware, budget→lucrative, alternatives/search→liquid+eager, commitment tests→all of it). **Niching usually wins:** fewer people, but higher self-awareness, budget, and differentiation.

## Product risk vs market risk — what conversations can and can't validate

Interviews de-risk **market** questions: *do they want it, will they pay, are there enough of them.* They do **not** validate **product** risk: *can you build it, can you grow it, will they keep using it.* For marketplaces, network-effect plays, ad/affiliate models, games, and most hard-tech, the dominant risk lives in the product — and the enthusiastic *"if you can get me X"* / *"if you can build it, I'd pay"* answer is **burden-shifting, not validation** (it reduces to "yes, I'd like more money / a better thing — obviously"). When an idea is product-risk-dominant, say so up front: conversations can confirm the market questions, but you'll have to start building earlier and with less certainty, and no volume of interviews substitutes for shipping. Treat any *"if you could…, I'd pay"* as a flag to dig, never a green light.

## Finding people to interview

**Ground rules:** target your ICP only (friends/family can't validate) · frame as **advice-seeking**, not selling ("I'm trying to have 20 conversations to give this a real shot — it'd mean the world if you'd help") · remove friction (calendar link, time slots) · expect low yield (**10% response is great** — don't take it personally) · **every interview ends with a referral ask** → one referral each = infinite supply.

Channels (you only need one or two to work):
- **Referrals** from your whole network; ask "who else?" at the end of every call.
- **LinkedIn** search by title+industry, personalized, offer to pay a fair fee. (Cohen/WP Engine: **40** cold emails to WordPress consultants → **all 40** agreed to a call → **30** agreed to pay $50/mo before any product existed → **not one** asked to be paid for their time.)
- **Events / conferences** your ICP attends; a speaking slot buys instant credibility.
- **Online communities** (Reddit, Discord, Slack, FB groups) — give first by answering others, don't sell. Avoid firehoses (HN, Quora) where targeting is impossible.
- **Monitor complaints** — review sites (G2, Capterra, TrustRadius), social rants, "how do I ___?" posts. People taking action (switching, building workarounds) are best: *free to complain, expensive to act.*
- **Landing page + cheap paid traffic** on low-competition keywords; offer an incentive; put the scheduling link right on the page.
- **Job postings** reveal exact pain points in "requirements"; hiring managers will often chat.
- **Paid platforms** (User Interviews, Upwork) and **expert networks** (GLG, Third Bridge) for hard-to-reach Directors/VPs — filter hard for true ICPs.
- **Your own podcast / micro-tool / events** ("invite my ICP to talk about themselves for 30 min" → waiting list) — slower but compounds into a marketing channel.

Goal of every interview: get **facts, commitment, advancement** — and either invalidate the idea or change it until it's validated.

### The outreach message — Vision / Framing / Weakness / Pedestal / Ask

The message that actually lands the meeting has five beats (mnemonic: *Very Few Wizards Properly Ask*):
- **Vision** — a half-sentence on how you're making some corner of the world better.
- **Framing** — where you're at and what you're looking for ("still early, trying to figure out if this is even worth building").
- **Weakness** — a specific gap you need help with (this gives them a real role, not a pitch to sit through).
- **Pedestal** — show that *they specifically* can fill it ("you've run X for years").
- **Ask** — ask for help, concretely (a 20-minute call this week).

**Don't open with** "Can I interview you?", "I'd love your opinion on what we're building", or "Got time for a quick coffee?" — these read as a pitch or a favor and tank the response rate. Frame it as *you* seeking *their* hard-won expertise.

## First marketing channel

Operate at the **intersection of (a) where your customers already are** and **(b) what comes to you naturally and joyfully.** Don't "get out of your comfort zone" — a channel you despise leaks through as inauthenticity, and being the *3rd biggest creator on a niche platform beats being 200th on a giant one.* Pick **one** channel and go deep before adding adjacent ones (each weak channel adds risk). If the intersection is empty — customers live only on a channel you'll never love — that may be the wrong business for you, or you need a co-founder whose superpower is that channel.

Ask: Where do they already spend time (search terms, podcasts, YouTube, communities, who they ask for recommendations)? Which medium do I genuinely want to master? Where could I realistically rank near the top?

## Note-taking signal symbols (from the book's cheatsheet)

Use while capturing interviews: `:)` excited · `:(` angry · `:|` embarrassed · ☇ pain/problem · ⨅ goal/job-to-be-done · ☐ obstacle · ⤴ workaround · ^ background/context · ☑ feature request or buying criterion · ＄ money/budget/buying process · ♀ named a specific person/company · ☆ follow-up task.

**Commitment currencies** (what a real "yes" costs them): meaningful **time**, **reputation** (an intro, a referral), or **money** (deposit, pre-order). Compliments and "keep me posted" cost nothing — they are not validation.
